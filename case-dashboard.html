Formation Insights case

We got into the user's mind and gave them what they wanted, even though they couldn't articulate it.

A dense operations surface has to support confidence, not just visibility. This project focused on helping managers read the system faster, orient themselves quicker, and act sooner.

My Role

Senior Product Designer responsible for research, product framing, UX structure and delivery support.

Product context

Geoscientists needed a complex analytical tool, that could proved them a complete information about earth formations.

The Challenge

Geoscientists and analysts previously relied on a fragmented ecosystem of third-party tools, each providing only a partial view of the data. Our goal was to consolidate these disparate data slices into a single, unified platform to streamline their workflow.

Outcome

Engineered an all-in-one interface for analyzing earth formations, drastically reducing the complexity of data comparison. Our solution's high efficiency led to successful partnerships with market giants like Tecpetrol and Baker Hughes.

How the problem looked

Geoscientists were working across multiple disconnected tools, each showing only one slice of the subsurface picture. To compare formations, well performance, lithology, and drilling indicators, they had to constantly switch between systems, manually align data, and build their own mental model from fragmented inputs.

This made analysis slow, cognitively demanding, and error-prone.

Even experienced users were spending too much time stitching information together instead of interpreting it. As a result, critical formation insights were harder to spot, and cross-well comparison required far more effort than it should have.

What users actually needed

Users did not need “more data” — they needed a way to see complex geological and drilling information in one coherent analytical environment. They needed to compare multiple wells side by side, track formation behavior across different views, and understand deviations or patterns without jumping between tools.

Most importantly, they needed a product that reduced interpretation overhead.

The right solution had to turn scattered technical signals into a clear, navigable workflow: one where users could move from raw formation data to confident operational insight faster, with less manual effort and less room for mistakes

On this photo, we can see how many different vizualizations it may take to make a correct data-based decision. Some geoscientist, engineers, dispatchers, drilling operators needed most of these power within one single application. This was our task.

Hypotheses before research

Users already have all the data. So they need clarity across scales

Analysis happens across multiple levels simultaneously. Geoscientists zoom from a top-down view of drilling geography to an “inside-the-well” perspective of formation behavior. A good interface must support this shift seamlessly, without forcing users to mentally reconstruct context between views.

Insights emerge from comparison, not isolation. Users constantly compare wells: drilling speed, formation response, and operational issues across layers. Only by aligning this data side-by-side can they understand what “normal” looks like — and where the subject well deviates.

The goal is not to show everything (interviews told us they don’t need it), but to reduce uncertainty. Users track how actual drilling performance differs from the plan, where issues concentrate, and how formations behave across wells. The system should highlight deviations, patterns, and next points of attention — so users can move from data to decisions faster. And what’s important, all of that data should be close to each other, within next tab preferably.

I acted as a translation layer between product intent and user reality. That meant clarifying what users meant by “more visibility,” converting that into interface priorities, and helping the team align around a calmer, more decision-oriented structure.

Let’s talk about some particular solutions.

Corelator view

Corelator view allows geoscientists to anchor multiple wells on a single screen, synchronized by depth. Thus enabling users to visually trace how drilling parameters deviate across different locations while maintaining a unified geological baseline.

In this tab users can add up to 5 wells and compare their parameters, based on depth (as was said above).

Formation thickness lines

These lines show users difference in depth (thickness) of formation in every selected well. Our design was pretty innovative and liked by users, so it had been copied later by some of our competitors.

This display of "pinch and swell" (thickness variation) of formations between wells allows geologists to immediately understand stratigraphic trends. Current formation is always highlighted.

Relative performance dashboard

One of user pains was that they need to «jump» between apps to compare key parameters for every well and then correlate it with wells depth based info. We assemble it all at the same place.

This panel is designed as a Relative Performance Dashboard. This "Leaderboard" approach instantly identifies the highest and lowest parameters among the selected wells, accelerating the decision-making process for operational optimization. Subject well - the object of analytics - is always highlighted to it’s place within the range.

Current formation reminder

User can highlight any formation in the chart area, this makes it possible for them to lose the current formation. So we always show the current formation in its separate area. This place in the right corner was selected based on interviews with users - they needed reminder of a current formation near their NPT events.

Formation Story calls the pop-up with the log of changes in data set.

NPT events

NPT’s are events that happened during drilling. E.g. bit stuck or a portion of liquifier poured.

Every event has it’s core and color. Similar to warning lights in a car dashboard. This color coding for NPTs is universal across all our apps. Users often just take a glance at how many NPTs are there and what’s their color. That’s how they read it.

Top-down view panel

Users sometimes need to see wells from above. This panel opens the top-down view. More details on that in next case. But before that app, users opened a top-down map of wells in a separate program, it surprised us that this was an industry-standard before. Users aproved the top-down map inside correlator during testing.

Depth selector

Another feature that was introduced - was a convenient depth selector. Transparent area within selected range shows marking under it, which helps users to navigate, when scale is too granular. Range can be changed. And also fragged along the axis to quickly navigate the entire dataset.

Lithologies

These weird area fills are called «lithologies». They represend various earth formations and every company has a set of their own lithology schemes. They all are customizable. All we can infuence here - is brightness. So we utilised that to show the active actual formation. Based on interviews, users sometimes "lost" the current formation among others, so they wanted to be more focused on it.

Gun Barrel View

Gun Barrel View shows complex 3D coordinates from the "drill’s point of view." This model allows operators to visualize the borehole's position relative to target formation layers in real time.

Colors mean other wells (so called «Offset wells»). Vertical and horizontal lines represent vertical and horizontal drift. The varying thickness along these lines (density markers) visualizes "residency time" - showing where the drill spent the majority of its operation. The round circle is the planned area, and it allows to understand whether we are deviating from the plan. Name is the name of a well.

Top Down View

Dots on the map represent other wellbores in the ares. Lines and their length represent to direction and length of the wellbore.

The "Top Down View" serves as a spatial validator. By showing the directional vectors of each well, the interface allows the user to instantly determine the relevance of a reference well. If a neighboring well is oriented differently, the scientist knows to weigh its data less heavily, preventing errors in geological interpretation.

Formation anchors

Gun barrel view tells operators where every wellbore is situated regarding the earth formations. These lines works as anchor points for users to understand how well is subject well directed.

Formation Story and parameter panel

Understanding a wellbore’s direction, length, and depth is not enought for a comprehensive evaluation. Users must also contextualize performance within specific wellbore’s circumstances. Our research revealed that a peroper analysis requires three data layers to be sychronized: Top-Down view, Gun Barrel, and Well Parameters - all integrated into a single, unified workspace.

This represents the second most critical view within the 'Formation Insights' platform.

Card reading logic

Order of reading for every card in this panel goes as following: Name of parameter > color of Δ to Avg. > Δ to Avg. value. And then, if «Δ to Avg.» goes beyond expectations, the user pays attention to the comparison columns of the characteristics.

Design of cards in this panel is dedicated to this reading logic.

Subject well hierarchy

The design utilizes a clear Target-vs-Reference hierarchy. The "Subject Well" is highlighted as the primary focal point, while "Offset Wells" are color-coded as reference data. This helps user to maintain his attention on comparing offset wells to subject well and make predictions on where to move next - informed.

Dynamic filter panel

Unlike previous view, this one needed our standard filter panel to do a dynamic attribute mapping. Users can assign specific metrics (e.g., "Number of Stages" or "12-month Production") to visual properties like bubble size or color gradients or quickly select batch of data they want to analize. We wanted to make this view a high-fidelity diagnostic engine.

Tracker operational table

This interface is designed as a depth-driven operational table, where each row represents a geological formation along the well trajectory. The layout mirrors the physical structure of the borehole, allowing users to navigate drilling progress in a spatially meaningful way.

Formation and subzone context

The left section visualizes formations and subzones using color-coded lithology and gamma ray data, helping users quickly associate numerical values with geological context. The vertical arrangement reflects true depth progression, making it easier to understand where the drill bit is relative to surrounding layers.

Current drill position

The current drill position is highlighted across all columns, providing a clear focal point. Formations above it are visually de-emphasized, indicating completed sections, while upcoming formations remain fully visible. This creates a natural distinction between past, present, and future states of the operation.

Depth and progress planning

Depth and progress columns combine actual measurements with projections. Users can see true vertical depth, measured depth, and lithology alongside estimated time to reach upcoming formations. This allows them to anticipate transitions and prepare for changes in drilling conditions.

Metrics assessment

The Metrics section compares planned and actual values for key parameters such as WOB, ROP, Torque, and RPM. Each value is supported by a visual bar and color-coded status, making deviations immediately noticeable. This enables quick assessment of whether the operation is performing within expected thresholds.

NPT events by formation

NPT events are displayed per formation. Tags and grouped indicators provide visibility into non-productive time without overwhelming the main dataset. Users can quickly identify where issues occurred and correlate them with specific geological intervals.

Row alignment context

All columns are aligned by row, ensuring that geological data, depth, time, performance metrics, and operational events are always viewed within the same contextual unit. This allows users to analyze relationships between variables without switching views or mentally reconstructing context.

Impact

We wanted geoscientists to compare wells, formations, and drilling behaviour inside one coherent analytical workspace.

Faster comparative analysis.

Users no longer had to jump between separate tools to understand formations, offsets, and drilling metrics. The app reduced setup time before actual analysis could begin.

Higher interpretation confidence.

Because geological context, well parameters, and NPT signals were aligned in one place, users could validate assumptions faster and make fewer interpretation mistakes.

A workflow that matched real work.

The interface started reflecting how geoscientists actually reason: across wells, across scales, and across time. That made the product feel immediately more useful during demos and trials.

Business outcome

The product became easier to sell because its value was visible in minutes, not after a long explanation.

By unifying scattered geological and operational data into one system, we turned a technically strong but fragmented idea into a convincing product story. The result helped position the solution for enterprise conversations and contributed to partnerships with companies such as Tecpetrol and Baker Hughes.

Reflection

The key shift came when we focused less on adding information and more on helping users keep context while moving between scales.

This case made one thing clear for me: in complex industrial products, usefulness does not come from density alone. It comes from helping experts keep context while moving between macro and micro views. Once we designed for that, the structure of the product became much easier to define.
