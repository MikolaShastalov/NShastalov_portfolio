Stream Quality case

Why seeing the real users within their real environment is crucial

I worked end-to-end on a monitoring tool for drilling teams that depend on real-time sensor streams. The challenge was not only to show data, but to help teams judge whether the data itself could be trusted before making expensive operational decisions.

My Role

As a Senior Product Designer, led the entire product development from discovery to post-delivery support.

Client context

Equinor is a Norwegian energy giant, oil and gas producer

What happened

Through research, I discovered a user pain point that was not known before, so we developed an app to solve the problem.

Outcome

We sold the product to ADNOC, Baker Hughes, Equinor.

How User pain has been discovered

Conducting research, I was present in a dispatcher room near a drilling site, sitting with one of the engineers. A colleague across the room asked him for the weight on bit (WOB) for a specific well. Having grown familiar with the software interface, I glanced at the engineer's screen and spotted the value immediately. It took me 1-2 seconds.

However, the engineer didn't answer right away. Instead of looking where I was looking, he began cycling through several different applications. I found myself wondering, "Is it possible he doesn't know where the weight on bit is displayed?"

After about ten seconds, he finally provided an answer, but the figure he gave was slightly different from what I saw on the screen.

The insight: I followed up with an interview to understand his thought process. He explained that sensors on a well aren't always reliable and frequently provide "noisy" or incorrect data. Consequently, engineers don't take the dashboard at face value. Instead, they cross-reference data across multiple apps, look for indirect signs to verify the data quality, and apply manual corrections based on their own experience.

That ten-second delay wasn't confusion; it was a manual validation process. This revealed a critical insight: our users are forced to act as "human filters" for unreliable sensor data. We now have a clear mission to solve this by automating data validation or improving data integrity at the source.

How I think

We need an app, that's entire purpose is to help them immediately understand if they can trust data from their sensors.

They think in patterns, so visualization should be pattern-first, instead of raw values

They already can evaluate data, so we need to offer them an "at-a-glance-ification" of this process.

User's double-check each others data quality, so we need to make the interface readable from afar by anyone in the room

The evolution of my understanding of the problem

Let’s talk about some particular solutions.

our main working space

Temporal Heatmap Visualization (Swimlane Pattern). Instead of traditional line graphs, we utilize multi-row status ribbons. This design choice allows the user to perceive not just a single error, but the duration and frequency of issues across multiple channels simultaneously. It transforms a complex time series into a scannable visual code: solid blue represents stability, while color-coded fragments represent specific incident types.

Categorical Segmentation (Functional Swimlanes). The interface is vertically partitioned into logical blocks (Uniformity, Sensibility, Completeness, etc.). These Categorical Swimlanes allow an operator to instantly isolate a problematic domain. For instance, if "Sensibility" shows heavy fragmentation while "Format" remains solid, the user can immediately narrow their diagnostic focus without being distracted by irrelevant data.

The thin vertical hairline is a critical tool for cross-parameter correlation. As the user hovers, the system aligns data across all categories. This solves the "context gap" by showing exactly what was happening in "Uniformity" at the precise moment "Sensibility" spiked, enabling rapid forensic analysis.

High-Density Information Layering. In sections like "Sensibility" and "Completeness," we display dozens of individual sub-channels. By using micro-line height and high-contrast color blocks, the design maximizes "data-to-pixel ratio." This allows the monitoring of a massive array of sensors on a single screen without the need for excessive vertical scrolling, which is vital for real-time mission-control environments.

Contextual Data Overlay (The Rich Tooltip). The Tooltip is designed as a deep-dive micro-dashboard. It doesn't just repeat the status; it provides specific telemetry values. By retaining the color markers within the tooltip, we maintain a strong visual scent, ensuring the user never loses track of which quality pillar they are investigating.

Leveraged Pre-attentive Processing. By making "All Good" (the blue baseline) the dominant background color, any anomaly acts as a visual disruptor. This leverages the human brain's ability to detect patterns and breaks in patterns almost instantly. This "Exception-Based Design" significantly reduces the Mean Time to Detect (MTTD) by drawing the eye only to the areas that require intervention.

The header serves as a summary executive layer. We know this is the part of the interface that users access most often, but also want to spend the least amount of time on. That's why we made it easy to read without taking up space on the main workspace: we adhere to the F-pattern. User can instantly assess the data’s health before diving into the granular complexity of the visualization below.

This app has 3 available «views». Every one was selected, based on user’s research and serves the various analytical approaches. Bubble chart is the last one - allows user to evaluate the quality of data streams, by seeing the dynamics of important parameter - ISLU (increase since the last update).

Position of Bubble tells the time and depth of Issue, color - type of issue and a small opaque bubble inside the bigger one shows the increase («I» in ISLU).

The numeric labels act as unique identifiers, allowing for quick cross-referencing with logs or reports. The semi-transparent overlapping of bubbles (Alpha-blending) prevents "data masking," ensuring that smaller events hidden behind larger ones remain visible.

Legend colors are based on colors, that are already associated by those types of issues in that customer’s team.

Impact

We wanted to sell the product to more customers, so we had to make sure that it satisfied the user as much as possible.

Detection speed.

Customer informed us that "on-time reactions" increased by ~18%.

Trust change.

After a year of use, the customer reported that the accuracy of problem detection had increased by approximately 20%.

Behavior change.

After a year of use, the client said that the number of untimely resolved problems had decreased by a quarter.

Business outcome

The solution was adopted by 3 big customers - ADNOC, Baker Huges and the initial customer - Equinor.

The product proved strong enough to move beyond the initial customer and become a commercially credible solution in a demanding industrial domain.

Reflection

The biggest leap ahead was to finally understand how users think

Once we stopped prioritizing patterns/values, detailed and superficial analysis, the work became clear and fast.
